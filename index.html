Caesar Is Dead — Stabbed By His Own Friends

Written By: Quintus Horatio & Valeria Maxima

Julius Caesar, Dictator of Rome
(100–44 B.C.)

Rome's most powerful leader was killed yesterday by a group of senators who called themselves "the liberators."

The whole city was shook.

"Et tu, Brute?"

Caesar's last words when he saw his close friend Brutus raise his dagger.

Yesterday was one of the worst days in Roman history. Julius Caesar, the leader of all of Rome, was stabbed and killed inside the Senate building by a group of senators who had secretly planned his murder for weeks. He was stabbed 23 times. The whole city went quiet when word spread through the streets.

Caesar had been warned. A soothsayer told him days before to "beware the Ides of March." His wife Calpurnia begged him to stay home after she had terrible nightmares. But Caesar refused to listen. He said he was not afraid, and he went to the Senate anyway.

"Cowards die many times before their deaths; the valiant never taste of death but once."

When he arrived, a group of senators crowded around him like wolves surrounding their prey. (Simile — compares the senators to wolves to show how dangerous and sneaky they were.) They pretended to have a petition for him. Then Casca struck first. Others quickly followed.

Caesar tried to fight back, but when he saw his close friend Brutus raise a dagger, he stopped. He pulled his toga over his face and fell to the ground right at the base of Pompey's statue. It was almost poetic that Caesar, who had once defeated Pompey in battle, fell at the feet of his old rival's statue. (Irony.)

The senators ran out shouting "Liberty! Freedom!" But nobody in the streets was cheering. People were scared and confused. Many ran home and locked their doors. By nightfall, Rome felt like a ghost town.

More Stories

The Killers Ran to Capitol Hill But Nobody Cheered for Them

Brutus and Cassius thought people would celebrate. They were wrong.

By Quintus Horatio — City Reporter

After the killing, Brutus and the other conspirators ran through the streets toward Capitol Hill. They were waving their bloody daggers and shouting that they had freed Rome. They expected people to cheer and thank them. Instead, everyone just stared. Some people ran away.

It was like showing up to a party and realizing nobody actually wanted you there. (Simile.)

Brutus tried to give a speech, but the crowd was not impressed. Mark Antony was already moving quickly across the city. He got hold of Caesar's body and his will. Sources say Caesar's will names his young nephew Octavian as his heir, which could change everything about who runs Rome next.

Antony Takes Charge and Plans a Big Public Funeral for Caesar

Caesar's closest ally moves fast to honor his fallen friend.

By Livia Claudia — Politics Reporter

Mark Antony did not waste any time after Caesar's death. He got Caesar's body, his private papers, and his will — all within hours of the killing. Now he is planning a huge public funeral in the Roman Forum where he will speak to the Roman people directly.

People close to Antony say his speech will be very powerful. "He is going to speak as a friend who lost someone he loved," one helper told our paper. Thousands of Romans are expected to show up. Shops are already closing. Schools are shut.

Right now, Rome is like a held breath, waiting to see what happens next. (Metaphor.)

Caesar's Wife Says She Begged Him Not to Go

Calpurnia knew something bad was going to happen. Caesar just would not listen.

By Valeria Maxima — Home Reporter

Caesar's wife Calpurnia told our reporter that she had terrible nightmares the night before the killing. She dreamed that Caesar's statue was pouring blood and that people were bathing their hands in it. She begged her husband to stay home and say he was sick.

But Caesar was too proud to stay home. "He told me that brave people only die once," Calpurnia said, her eyes red from crying. "Cowards die over and over in their minds. He really believed that."

Her grief is like an ocean with no shore in sight. (Metaphor.) She is now staying at home on the Palatine Hill and is not speaking to anyone else.

Our Opinion

Editorial

Was Killing Caesar Really the Right Thing to Do?

By The Editorial Staff — The Roman Times

The men who killed Caesar are calling themselves heroes. They say they saved Rome from a dangerous ruler who wanted too much power. But here at The Roman Times, we have to ask — did they actually help Rome, or did they just make everything worse?

Yes, Caesar was powerful. Yes, some people were scared of him. But being powerful is not the same as being evil. The conspirators never put Caesar on trial. They never gave him a chance to defend himself. They just decided he had to die and went through with it. That is not justice. That is just murder with a fancy excuse.

The fact that they call themselves "liberators" after doing something so violent is a perfect example of irony.

Rome has been through a lot. But stabbing your own leader in the back is not the way to fix things. Ambition is like fire — useful when it warms you, but dangerous when it burns everything down. (Simile.) These men lit a match and called it freedom. History has to decide if they were right.

Obituaries

Julius Caesar, Age 55
Dictator, General, Leader of Rome

Julius Caesar was one of the greatest leaders Rome has ever seen. He won wars in Gaul, Britain, and across the known world. He crossed the Rubicon River in 49 B.C. and changed history forever.

He was strict but also generous. He forgave many of his enemies, including Brutus himself. Without Caesar, Rome feels like a city that lost its heartbeat. (Hyperbole — used to show how deeply Rome will feel his absence.)

He is survived by his wife Calpurnia and his nephew Octavian.

Portia, Age 34
Wife of Brutus, Daughter of Cato

Portia was a brave and loyal wife. She knew something was bothering her husband Brutus and begged him to tell her what was wrong. She even stabbed herself in the leg just to prove she could keep a secret, showing just how much she loved and trusted him.

Portia was strong, smart, and devoted. She deserved better than the fate that the conspiracy brought to her family. She will be remembered as one of Rome's most courageous women.

Calpurnia's Peace of Mind
Wife of Caesar — Voice That Went Unheard

Calpurnia is still alive, but the life she knew is gone. She tried to save her husband. She warned him, she begged him, she practically blocked the door. Nobody listened.

Now she is left behind to grieve a man who thought he was too great to be destroyed. Her dreams were right. Her warning was real. If Caesar had just listened, everything could have been different. That is the hardest kind of loss — the kind you saw coming.

This situation is an example of dramatic irony — the audience knows something bad is going to happen even though the character does not.

Human Interest

A Mother Waits for Her Son to Come Home from Caesar's Wars

For one Roman family, the news of Caesar's death means a future full of questions.

By Flavia Serena — Human Interest

On a small street near the Tiber River, a woman named Marcia has been waiting for her son Marcus to come back from fighting in Caesar's army. He left two years ago. She gets a letter every few months, but the last one came back in December.

Now Caesar is dead, and Marcia does not know what that means for her son. Will the army keep going? Will he still have a job? Will he even come home?

"Caesar gave boys like my son a reason to fight. He made them feel like they were part of something big and important."

This references how Caesar, like the great heroes of Roman mythology, inspired loyalty and courage in ordinary people. (Allusion to Roman heroic tradition.) She sat quietly for a moment, then said, "I just want my son to come home safe. That is all I have ever wanted."

The Man Who Sells Bread in the Forum Saw Everything Happen

An ordinary Roman watched history unfold from behind his bread cart.

By Lucius Granus — Human Interest

Gaius has been selling bread in the Roman Forum for 18 years. He has seen a lot of crazy things. But he said yesterday was unlike anything he has ever experienced in his life.

"I heard shouting, and then senators came running past my cart. Some of them still had blood on their hands," he said, shaking his head. "Then people in the crowd started screaming. It spread through the Forum like fire through dry leaves." (Simile — compares the panic spreading to how quickly fire spreads.)

He did not sell a single loaf of bread after noon. He just stood there and watched Rome fall apart. "Caesar used to walk through here sometimes," he said quietly. "He would nod at us regular folks. He knew we were here." He paused and looked at the empty Forum. "I wonder who is going to walk through here next."
