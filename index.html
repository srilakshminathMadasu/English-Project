CAESAR SLAIN ON THE IDES OF MARCH

The Dictator Perpetuo falls to a band of Senators; the Eternal City trembles as the fate of the Republic hangs by a thread upon this most dreadful day

By Gaius Scribonius · Senate Correspondent · Published This Morning

Julius Caesar, Dictator Perpetuo of Rome, was this morning assassinated within the Theatre of Pompey, falling before an assembly of some twenty-three Senators armed with daggers concealed beneath their togas. The attack came as Caesar took his seat, at which moment the conspirators descended upon him with sudden and terrible violence.

Sources present at the scene report that Casca struck the first blow, followed swiftly by the others. Caesar is said to have defended himself briefly before recognizing among his attackers the face of Marcus Junius Brutus — at which moment he ceased to struggle and drew his toga about his face to meet his end with the dignity of a Roman.

"Et tu, Bruté? — Then fall, Caesar."
— Last words attributed to Gaius Julius Caesar, Theatre of Pompey

The body bore three-and-twenty wounds, though physicians report that but a single wound to the chest proved fatal. The Senate hall fell into immediate panic, with citizens fleeing into the streets.

Brutus Addresses the Citizens: "Rome is Free"

By Publius Velius · Forum Reporter

Marcus Junius Brutus descended to the Forum Romanum and addressed the populace, defending the actions of those he called liberators. He declared the deed was done not out of malice toward Caesar the man, but out of love for Rome and fear of tyranny.

The crowd appeared briefly swayed by Brutus's eloquent reasoning. He spoke of honour, of the Republic, and of the freedoms imperilled by one man's unchecked ambition — drawing comparisons to the expulsion of King Tarquinius Superbus of centuries past.

"Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved Rome more."
— Brutus, Forum Romanum

Antony's Oration Turns the Tide of Opinion

By Flavia Maxima · Forum Correspondent

Mark Antony, granted the right to speak at Caesar's funeral by a perhaps overly trusting Brutus, delivered an oration that those present shall not soon forget. Beginning with the words now repeated throughout Rome — "Friends, Romans, countrymen" — he spoke with calculated and devastating precision.

Antony repeatedly styled Brutus an "honourable man" with an irony so sharp as to provoke the assembled citizens to fury. He displayed Caesar's bloodied toga, and read aloud a will bequeathing Caesar's gardens to the people and seventy-five drachmas to every Roman citizen.

By the time Antony had finished, the mob had turned entirely against the conspirators. Riots erupted; Brutus and Cassius fled Rome. Civil war, this correspondent fears, is inevitable.

Calpurnia's Warning and the Ignored Soothsayer

By Tertia Cornelia · Palace Correspondent

Those close to Caesar reveal that this morning was not without its warnings. Caesar's wife, Calpurnia, besought him urgently not to attend the Senate, having been visited in the night by dreams of dreadful portent — she saw his statue spouting blood, and Romans bathing their hands in it whilst smiling.

Caesar was briefly persuaded to remain at home, before Decius Brutus arrived and with flattery convinced the great man that Calpurnia had misread her dreams, and that the Senate awaited him with great honours.

Most fatally: a soothsayer had warned Caesar days prior. As Caesar passed him this morning, he called out — "The Ides of March are come." The soothsayer replied simply: "Aye, Caesar — but not gone."

ANALYSIS

The Ghost of Caesar and the Fall of the Conspirators

By Lucius Veranius · Senior Political Correspondent

In the days and weeks following the assassination, the faction of Brutus and Cassius found themselves not the liberators of Rome they imagined, but hunted men. Mark Antony, joined by the young Octavian — Caesar's adopted heir — and the general Lepidus, formed the Second Triumvirate and moved against the conspirators with overwhelming force.

The two armies met at the plains of Philippi in Macedonia. There, in two decisive engagements, the republican forces were broken. Cassius, believing mistakenly that Brutus had been defeated, fell upon his own sword. Brutus, after suffering a final defeat, followed suit — declaring that Caesar's ghost had at last been avenged.

Thus did the conspirators' enterprise end not in the restoration of the Republic they so dearly wished to preserve, but in its further destruction. The men who killed Caesar to prevent a monarchy gave Rome an emperor.

⚡ Omens & Portents ⚡

Citizens report that in the days preceding the Ides, numerous terrible omens were observed throughout Rome. A soothsayer warned Caesar directly: "Beware the Ides of March."

Caesar's wife Calpurnia was visited by dreadful dreams of his statue spouting blood. Lions were reportedly seen whelping in the streets. Graves yielded up their dead. Above the Capitol, fiery warriors fought in the clouds.

Caesar dismissed all such warnings, declaring he was not afraid of death. He was perhaps the only man in Rome who was not.

Portia's Sacrifice

Brutus's devoted wife Portia, having been privy to the conspiracy and unable to bear her husband's disgrace and flight, is reported to have taken her own life. Rome mourns her.

The Young Octavian

Caesar's great-nephew and adopted son returns to Rome to claim his inheritance. Observers note an uncommon shrewdness in the young man that belies his eighteen years.

Cicero's Silence

Notably absent from the conspiracy despite his republican sympathies, Cicero was not informed of the plot in advance — the conspirators feared his caution would have forestalled them.

TIMELINE

The Play: Act by Act

ACT I

Tribunes chastise Caesar's celebrating crowd. The soothsayer warns of the Ides. Cassius begins seducing Brutus toward conspiracy, appealing to his republican ideals.

ACT II

Brutus, tormented, resolves to join the plot. Portia pleads with him. Caesar ignores Calpurnia's dreams and Artemidorus's letter of warning, and proceeds to his doom.

ACT III

The assassination. Antony's devastating funeral oration. The mob erupts in riot. Brutus and Cassius are forced to flee Rome entirely.

ACT IV

The Triumvirate consolidates power. Brutus and Cassius quarrel then reconcile. The Ghost of Caesar visits Brutus, promising to meet him at Philippi.

ACT V

The Battle of Philippi. Both Cassius and Brutus die by their own hands. Antony eulogises Brutus as "the noblest Roman of them all."

THEMES

Central Themes

Shakespeare's tragedy compels its audience to grapple with questions as urgent today as in Caesar's Rome. Chief among these is the tension between personal honour and political necessity — embodied in Brutus, who kills a friend out of principle and destroys himself in the process.

The play also asks: what makes a tyrant? Caesar is ambitious, yes — but is ambition alone sufficient cause for murder? Cassius's motivations are revealed as far less noble than Brutus's, suggesting the conspiracy is rotten at its core.

Power corrupts not only its holders but those who would remove it. Antony, ostensibly avenging Caesar, proves himself every bit as manipulative as Cassius. The Republic was doomed regardless of the Ides of March.

Most haunting: the play's treatment of fate. Soothsayers, omens, and ghosts suggest Caesar's end was written long before any conspirator raised a blade.
